Welcome to the digital diary of Stephanie Rachmeler.

Who Am I?

Isn't it interesting that that question is often answered with our profession? We are defined by what we do. I am a graphic designer. Recently, I was contracting with a company for almost two years when the funds ran out and I was laid off. While that doesn't change my abilities or knowledge, it still felt like a hit to me as a person. "Am I still a designer when I'm not designing (on a consistent basis)?"

Why Am I Here?

In this involuntary time of discovery, I have realized a lot about myself. I wrongly assumed that, at 33, I had nothing new to learn about myself. I have varying interests, all creative in one way or another, and there were times I felt schizophrenic because I couldn't commit to one. I now realize the one unifying theme: Simplicity.

Feeling Limited

I have resisted coding and the digital world for as long as possible (we're almost living with the Jetsons and I still want to read the news on paper). While I enjoy a well-designed digital experience, I dislike its intangibility. While I still love paper and other tactile mediums, I'm tired of my digital presence being limited to what I can manipulate in Squarespace or Adobe Portfolio.

Visual Clutter

I have found that visual clutter in my home had started to affect my ability to concentrate. I was always distracted, because in the back of my mind was the thought "Oh, I need to do X later." There is currently a rise in the minimalist approach to living, as seen in the Netflix documentary Minimalism and the popularity of the book The Life-Changing Magic of Tidying Up, among others.

The Lightbulb Moment

Reducing the clutter (recycling old magazines, culling my closet) and the joy it brought me have now permeated my work life as well. Not only do I think clearer, but I want to impart that same feeling to my clients through my work. Once I realized that coding is 100% organization, a lightbulb went on. It took me a minute but I am now very excited to learn to code.
